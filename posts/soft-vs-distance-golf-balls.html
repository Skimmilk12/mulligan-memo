The short answer

"Soft" describes how a ball feels (low compression); "distance" is a marketing badge usually stuck on the same kind of firm, low-spin two-piece ball. The myth to drop first: softer does not mean longer — independent robot tests keep finding the softest balls are among the shortest off the driver. The real fork isn't soft vs distance, it's ionomer cover vs urethane cover. Pick soft for feel and comfort, firmer/premium urethane for greenside control, and if you lose a sleeve a round, buy cheap and play it without fear.

Walk the ball aisle and you'll see two words fighting for your money: soft and distance. The pitch writes itself — soft is gentle and forgiving, distance is long and macho, and somewhere in the middle is the "right" ball for you. Here's the honest take after years of watching golfers agonize over this exact shelf: those two labels are mostly marketing stuck onto remarkably similar balls, and the single most common belief about them — that a softer ball flies farther, especially at slower swing speeds — is backwards. Independent robot testing keeps showing the opposite.

So this guide isn't going to crown "soft" or "distance" the winner, because that's the wrong question. The right questions are: what do you actually want this ball to do, how many do you lose per round, and do you care more about feel and price or about stopping the ball on the green? Answer those three honestly and the choice makes itself. If you want the wider lay of the land first, our golf ball buyer's guide covers covers, compression, and construction from the top down.

The LabelsWhat "soft" and "distance" really mean on the box

Start by separating what the words describe, because they aren't even measuring the same thing. "Soft" is a feel claim — it's pointing at compression, how much the ball squishes at impact. A low-compression ball gives a quieter, springier sensation off the putter and wedges. "Distance" is a performance claim aimed at the driver — it usually means a firm, low-spin, two-piece construction tuned for ball speed and roll.

Here's the catch most aisle signage hides: an awful lot of "soft" balls and "distance" balls are built the same way underneath — a big rubber core wrapped in a firm ionomer (Surlyn-type) cover. One box leans into "feels soft," the other leans into "goes far," and the actual ball in your hand is often a two-piece value ball either way. The genuinely meaningful difference between balls isn't the adjective on the front — it's the cover material, which we'll get to.

  • "Soft" = a feel/compression claim. Low compression, squishy feel off short clubs. Says nothing about distance or spin on its own.
  • "Distance" = a driver-performance claim. Firm, low-spin, built for ball speed and roll. Usually two-piece ionomer.
  • The real divide is the cover. Ionomer (most soft and distance balls) vs urethane (premium balls) decides greenside spin — and that's what actually changes your scoring.

Bust the MythMyth check: does a soft ball actually go farther?

This is the belief that costs golfers the most money and confidence, so let's be blunt about it. The popular logic goes: "I have a slower swing, so a soft, low-compression ball will compress more and give me extra yards." It's intuitive. It also doesn't survive contact with a robot.

Independent robot testing — the kind done by outlets like MyGolfSpy and Today's Golfer — has repeatedly found that the softest, lowest-compression balls tend to be among the shortest off the driver, not the longest. That's a third-party finding, not our measurement — Mulligan Memo doesn't run a robot or a launch monitor, and we're not going to pretend we do.

"Soft sells the feel in your hands. It does not sell the yards on the scorecard. Those are two different transactions."

How big is the penalty? Today's Golfer's robot work has reported driver-carry losses for the softest balls on the order of about 8 yards near 78 mph, around 11 yards near 93 mph, and up to roughly 15 yards near 114 mph swing speed — directional figures, not a precise promise for your swing, and Today's Golfer's third-party results rather than our own. The pattern matters more than any single number: across the speed range, soft was the slower, shorter option, and the gap actually widened for faster swingers. Nobody "compressed" their way to extra distance.

So if the only reason you're eyeing a soft ball is distance, the testing says stop — you're chasing yards that aren't there. The good reasons to play soft are real, but they're about feel, not flight.

The NumberCompression in plain English: the spec behind "feel"

Compression is the headline number behind the word "soft," so it's worth understanding without the jargon. It's essentially how much the ball deforms — squishes — when the clubface hits it. A low number feels soft and muted; a high number feels firm and "clicky." That's mostly it. Compression is a feel dial, not a distance dial.

One honest caveat before any numbers: manufacturers rarely publish official compression figures, so the values floating around come from third-party testing and vary by source and model year. Treat every number below as approximate and verify against a current reputable chart before you lean on it.

The useful takeaway isn't a target number; it's that compression tells you what the ball will feel like, and nothing reliable about how far it goes. Pick the feel you trust over the putter and wedges, and let distance be settled by the things that actually drive it — speed, launch, and spin together.

The Real ForkCover material matters more than "soft"

If you remember one thing from this whole guide, make it this: the spec that actually changes how a ball plays around the green is the cover material, not the compression number on the front of the box.

There are two camps. Premium balls use a soft urethane cover that grips into wedge grooves, so the ball bites and checks up on short shots. Most cheap soft balls and most distance balls use a firmer ionomer (Surlyn-type) cover that spins less, so the ball releases and runs out instead of stopping. Generally speaking — and there are exceptions creeping in — urethane equals greenside spin, ionomer equals roll-out.

This is exactly why "soft" can be so misleading. A soft ionomer ball feels lovely off the putter and still won't stop on a firm green, because softness (feel) and stopping power (cover) are unrelated. The cleanest example of a ball that blurs the old line is Callaway Chrome Soft, a soft-feeling ball with a genuine urethane cover — soft feel and greenside spin in one ball, though you pay premium money for the urethane, and the trade-off only narrows, it doesn't vanish. It's worth a caution here: not every "soft" ball with some greenside spin is urethane. Titleist Tour Soft, for instance, pairs a soft feel with a reformulated ionomer (Fusablend) cover for short-game grip — better than a basic value ball, but it isn't a urethane ball, so don't lump it in with the premium spinners.

Cover Feel Greenside spin Typical price Found on
Ionomer / Surlyn Firm to soft (varies) Lower — releases and rolls out Lower Most "soft" and "distance" value balls
Urethane Soft to firm (varies) Higher — grips and checks up Higher Premium / tour balls (incl. some "soft" ones)

So when you're standing in the aisle, ignore the adjective for a second and find the cover. That one fact predicts greenside behavior better than "soft," "distance," compression, or price.

Fit It To YouWho should play a soft ball (and who's wasting money on one)

Soft balls aren't a trap — they're a genuinely good fit for some golfers and a placebo for others. The honest split:

  • Good fit: golfers who simply prefer a soft, quiet feel off the putter and wedges and putt or chip with more confidence because of it. Feel is real, even if it isn't yards.
  • Good fit: players whose hands or joints don't love a firm, clicky ball — a softer feel is easier on the hands and more pleasant over four-plus hours.
  • Wasting money: anyone buying soft specifically to gain distance at a slow swing speed. The testing says it won't deliver that, and you may even be giving up a few yards.
  • Wasting money: golfers who think "soft" means the ball will stop on the green. If it's a cheap ionomer soft ball, it won't — that's a cover question, not a feel question.

The clean rule: choose soft for feel and comfort, never as a distance strategy. If you love how a Supersoft feels off your putter, that's a completely legitimate reason to play it — just buy it for the right reason.

The Other SideWho should play a distance or premium ball instead

Now the other two camps. A firmer distance ball makes sense if your priority is raw yards and a straight, roll-out flight, and you don't rely on the ball checking up around the green. It's also typically cheaper and more durable — a real advantage if you scuff covers or just don't want to spend up. (We go deep on this in our guide to the best golf balls for distance.)

A premium urethane ball makes sense when your scoring depends on the short game — when you can already generate spin and you want wedges and chips to bite and stop. That control is what you're paying up for, not extra driver yards. And here's the nuance the marketing won't volunteer: a few premium balls deliver soft feel and urethane spin together (Titleist AVX, Callaway Chrome Soft), so "soft" and "control" aren't mutually exclusive at the top of the range — they're just more expensive together. Premium urethane balls generally land somewhere in the roughly $50–$65 per dozen range at full retail, versus value soft and distance balls that often run under $30 — but discounts, multipacks, and "buy 3 get 1 free" deals move the real number around constantly, so always check the current price rather than trusting any figure printed here.

Honesty CornerSwing speed, feel, and the part marketing won't tell you

Two things the box won't say out loud. First, the slow-swing-speed pitch for soft balls is mostly mythology. The notion that a slower swinger "compresses" a soft ball into extra distance hasn't held up — in robot testing soft balls were still short even at slow speeds. If you swing slowly, choose your ball on feel and how it launches for you, not on a compression chart. (Our companion piece on the best golf balls for slow swing speeds separates the feel from the fact in detail.)

Second — and this cuts the other way — feel is genuinely worth paying for, even though it doesn't add a single yard. A softer ball gives a quieter, springier sensation off the putter and wedges that a lot of golfers honestly prefer and putt better with. That's not nothing. Confidence over a four-footer is a real performance input, even if no launch monitor measures it. So this isn't "soft is a scam" — it's "soft is a feel preference, priced and chosen as one." Buy the feel you like; just don't expect it to show up as distance.

DecideHow to actually pick: a 4-question buyer's checklist

Skip the adjectives and answer these four in order. They'll point you at a ball faster than any marketing claim.

  • 1. How many balls do you lose per round? If it's more than a couple, stop here — buy a cheap value 2-piece or recycled balls and move on. Fit matters little when you're feeding the woods a sleeve a round.
  • 2. Does your scoring depend on stopping the ball on the green? If yes, you need a urethane cover, full stop — soft or firm. If no, an ionomer ball is fine and cheaper.
  • 3. Do you care more about feel or about price? If feel, a soft ball (value or premium) is a fair buy. If price, a firm value ball does nearly everything the average golfer needs.
  • 4. Are you actually chasing distance? If so, don't buy "soft" for it — a firmer, low-spin distance ball is the longer choice for most players, per the testing.

Notice that not one of those questions is "soft or distance?" That label was never the real decision — it just sells boxes.

The Last WordBottom line: stop overthinking it

Here's the version I'd give a friend on the first tee. "Soft vs distance" is mostly a marketing fork, not a real one — both labels usually sit on similar two-piece balls, and softer does not mean longer no matter what the box implies. The choice that actually matters is the cover: ionomer if you want cheap, durable, and don't need the ball to stop; urethane if your scoring depends on greenside spin. Compression is a feel preference worth indulging, never a distance strategy.

FAQQuick answers

Do soft golf balls go farther than distance balls?

Usually no. Independent robot tests have repeatedly found the softest, lowest-compression balls are among the shortest off the driver, not the longest. "Soft" is about how the ball feels, not how far it flies — if pure distance is your goal, a firmer distance or premium ball is generally the longer choice.

Are soft golf balls better for slow swing speeds?

Not for distance — the popular idea that slow swingers "compress" a soft ball for extra yards hasn't held up in testing; soft balls were still short even at slow speeds. Where soft helps is feel and comfort: easier on the hands and a pleasant feel off the putter. So choose soft for feel, not for a yardage boost.

What's the real difference between a soft ball and a premium ball?

Two different things. "Soft" refers to low compression (feel), while a premium ball's edge is its urethane cover, which grips your wedges for more spin and stopping power around the green. Most cheap soft balls use a firmer ionomer/Surlyn cover that spins less — a few premium soft balls (like Chrome Soft or AVX) give you both, but you pay more.

What golf ball should a beginner buy — soft or distance?

Honestly, neither premium option at first. If you're losing several balls a round, buy an inexpensive value 2-piece ball or recycled balls and don't overthink soft vs distance. Once your ball loss drops, pick based on feel preference — try a soft ball and a firmer one and keep whichever you putt and chip better with.

Does compression really matter for the average golfer?

It matters for feel more than for scoring. Compression mostly changes how firm or soft the ball feels at impact; lower numbers feel softer, higher numbers feel firmer and "clicky." Real-world distance differences between balls are smaller than the marketing suggests, so pick the feel you like rather than chasing a compression number.

Will a softer ball help me stop the ball on the green?

Generally the opposite. Stopping power comes from a urethane cover, and most budget soft balls use a firmer ionomer cover that releases and rolls out more. If greenside spin matters to you, look at a urethane ball (soft or firm) rather than assuming "soft" equals "stops fast."

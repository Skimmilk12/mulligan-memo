Golf Simulator Shed Guide: Sizes, Ceiling Height, and What It Really Costs

The ceiling kills more simulator builds than the budget does. Here's the honest math on shed dimensions, launch monitor space, climate, power, and cost — before you pour a single dollar into the backyard.

The short answer

Retailer planning guides converge on a minimum golf simulator shed of roughly 10 ft wide × 14–15 ft deep × 8.5–9 ft high, with 12×20 ft the practical minimum footprint and 14–16 ft W × 18–25 ft D × 10 ft H the ideal. Budget for the shell, the interior fit-out, and the simulator as three separate line items — a realistic all-in range runs from around $10,000 to well past $40,000 as of July 2026.

A golf simulator shed exists to solve exactly one problem: almost nobody's house has the ceiling for a driver swing. Standard residential rooms top out at 8 ft, garage doors and their tracks eat clearance down to 7 ft or less, and the moment you take a full swing indoors you discover why the simulator industry publishes so many room-planning guides. Carl's Place's planning guidance says the average golfer needs a 9–10 ft ceiling to swing comfortably indoors, with 8 ft the bare minimum — and at 8 ft, "minimum" usually means wedges and irons only, no driver. So the build moves outside, into a purpose-sized building where you control every dimension. This guide walks through the sizes that actually work, the wall-height trap that catches shed buyers, what each launch monitor demands in depth, and what the whole project really costs.

Start HereWhy the ceiling decides everything

Before footprint, before budget, before anything: height. The planning pages from the three biggest simulator retailers all say roughly the same thing, with slightly different comfort thresholds:

  • Carl's Place: the average golfer needs 9–10 ft to swing indoors; 8 ft is the bare minimum, which fits their shortest 7.7 ft enclosure but usually rules out the driver.
  • Shop Indoor Golf: 8 ft is the absolute minimum, 8.5 ft is more accommodating, and 9 ft or more is optimal.
  • Rain or Shine Golf: at least 9 ft high for a comfortable setup.

The consensus across all three: 10 ft is the comfortable standard for a full driver swing, 9 ft works for most golfers, and 8 ft is a compromise you should only accept knowingly. There is no single magic number — a 5'6" golfer with a flat swing has more margin than a 6'4" golfer with a steep one — which is why Rain or Shine's advice is the best pre-purchase test in this entire guide: take real, full-speed driver swings in the actual space (or a mocked-up equivalent) before you spend a dollar. Tape a box on the lawn, stand in it, and swing. If the club would hit a 9 ft ceiling, you need a 10 ft one.

Nobody has ever finished a simulator shed and complained that the ceiling was too high.

Golf simulator shed size: minimum, comfortable, and ideal

Width and depth get less press than height but sink just as many projects. Pulling together the published planning guidance from Carl's Place, Shop Indoor Golf, and Rain or Shine Golf, the numbers cluster into three tiers:

For a shed specifically, Carl's Place calls 12×20 ft the minimum viable footprint. That number makes sense once you do the depth math their shed guide lays out: roughly 1 ft of buffer behind the impact screen (Rain or Shine says 12–16 inches, so the ball doesn't ricochet the screen into the wall), 10–12 ft from ball to screen, and about 7 ft of swing and backswing room behind the golfer. Add it up and you're at roughly 18 ft of interior depth minimum — before a radar launch monitor asks for more. Guides that skip the screen buffer and the behind-you space produce sheds that are two or three feet too short, and that's not a mistake you can renovate your way out of.

The wall-height trap (and the insulation tax)

Here is where shed shopping diverges from room planning. A shed advertised as "10 ft tall" is almost always quoting the exterior peak of a gabled or gambrel roof. The side walls — where your clubhead actually travels at the top of the backswing and through the follow-through — might be only 6 or 7 ft. Unless you're hitting from dead center under the ridge with a perfectly on-plane swing, wall height is the number that matters, not peak height. When you get quotes, ask for the interior wall height specifically, and ask what the ceiling looks like once it's finished.

Related trap: Carl's Place notes that insulation and interior finishing shave inches off every dimension. A 12 ft-wide shell framed with 2×4 walls, insulated, and finished loses several inches per side; a 10 ft ceiling with insulation and sheathing overhead is no longer 10 ft. Spec the shed so the finished interior hits your numbers, not the bare shell.

How much depth does your launch monitor add?

This is the spec most first-time builders miss entirely, and it's why a generic "15 ft deep is fine" claim can be flat wrong. Launch monitors come in two architectures with completely different space demands. Radar units sit behind the ball and need a corridor: distance from sensor to tee, plus ball flight to the screen. Camera (photometric) units sit beside or just behind the ball and need almost no corridor at all. If your shed can't hit radar numbers, a camera unit rescues the build. Prefab vs. DIY vs. converting the shed you already have

Three routes into a backyard golf simulator shed, in ascending order of effort:

  • Prefab simulator-ready shells. A real category now: Sheds Unlimited (PA) builds simulator-configured sheds, Stoltzfus Structures / mysheds.com lists golf sheds, Golf2U quotes custom simulator sheds, and Sim Shacks (Toronto area) sells a turnkey package including permits, site prep, electrical, install, and calibration. We haven't toured or tested any of these — they're named as proof the route exists, not as reviews. Sheds Unlimited's golf-shed guide prices simulator-ready prefab shells at roughly $12,000–$26,668+ for the shell alone, and turnkey luxury prefab simulator buildings run ~$70,000–$97,000+.
  • DIY or basic shell. Generic shed-guide estimates put a basic DIY shell at ~$3,000–$8,000, and Carl's Place cites ~$4,500 as the average homeowner spend on a basic shed install. The catch: sheds arrive unfinished inside. Insulation, electrical, and flooring are all extra, and they're not small extras.
  • Convert an existing shed or garage. The cheapest route on paper and the most common failure in practice — existing buildings usually fail on height, not footprint. A garden shed with 6.5 ft walls cannot be talked into hosting a driver swing, and a golf simulator garage conversion lives or dies on whether the door tracks and ceiling give you 9 ft of true clearance. Measure first; many don't survive the tape measure.

Climate: condensation is the killer, not cold

The most persistent myth in backyard-simulator planning is that winter cold destroys electronics. The consensus across golf-simulator-forum threads and retailer guidance is more specific and more useful: electronics tolerate cold sitting still. What kills them is repeated warm-cold-warm cycling, which condenses moisture onto circuit boards and optics every time the building swings through the dew point. Projector bulbs are the most temperature-sensitive component in the whole system.

Two workable strategies, per that same guidance:

  • Insulate and climate-control. A 12,000 BTU ductless mini-split — roughly $500–$1,200 for DIY-friendly units — handles a 400–500 sq ft space, keeps the interior above the condensation cycle in winter, and cools it in summer. This is the "use it year-round like a real room" option.
  • Remove the electronics between sessions. Bring the launch monitor, PC, and projector inside after each use, and always let gear reach room temperature before powering on. Cheaper up front, more friction forever.

One more moisture note: impact screens can mildew in damp buildings. In humid climates, a dehumidifier is not optional equipment — a wavy, spotted screen ruins both the picture and the resale value of your enclosure.

Power, lighting, and network

Per Carl's Place's setup guidance: plan a dedicated 20-amp circuit for the projector, PC, and launch monitor, plus additional outlets for lighting and HVAC, and hire a licensed electrician to run it. Carl's Place specifically warns against daisy-chaining power strips — it's a fire risk, and an extension cord across the lawn from the house is the same idea with worse weatherproofing. While the trench is open for power, run hard-wired ethernet; simulator software updates, streaming, and multiplayer are all miserable over marginal backyard WiFi.

Lighting wants to be even and indirect: bright enough for the launch monitor's cameras to see the ball, positioned so nothing glares on the impact screen or shines into the unit's optics, and with no direct sunlight hitting the screen. On permits: requirements vary by municipality and by shed size — many jurisdictions exempt small sheds, but a 12×20+ building with electrical typically triggers permitting. Check your local building code before you order anything; some prefab vendors (Sheds Unlimited, Sim Shacks) offer permitting assistance as part of the sale.

What goes inside: the loadout

Once the building exists, the interior is a standard simulator build: launch monitor, impact screen and enclosure, hitting mat, projector, and a PC or tablet to run the software. Two placement notes specific to sheds: mount the projector so your shadow and your club never cross the beam, and leave that 12–16 inch buffer behind the screen even though every instinct says to push it flat against the back wall.

The mistakes that cost people their build

  • Buying the shed before measuring the swing. Rain or Shine's take-real-driver-swings test costs nothing. Skipping it can cost a five-figure building.
  • Reading peak height as usable height. The ridge doesn't matter; the walls do.
  • Skipping insulation and the vapor barrier. That's how you sign up for the condensation cycle and a mildewed screen.
  • Running power on an extension cord. Dedicated 20-amp circuit, licensed electrician, no daisy-chained strips — per Carl's Place, that last one is a fire risk, full stop.
  • No buffer behind the screen. 12–16 inches per Rain or Shine, or the ball drives the screen into the wall and ruins both.
  • Forgetting the launch monitor's corridor. A shed sized for a SkyTrak+ does not fit a Mevo+ at its preferred 21 ft. Match the building to the unit.

The Last Word

A backyard golf simulator shed is one of the few golf purchases where the boring homework genuinely is the whole game. Get the height right (10 ft comfortable, 9 ft workable, 8 ft a knowing compromise), get the depth right (18 ft interior covers the screen buffer, the ball flight, and your backswing), match the building to your launch monitor's corridor, and insulate against the condensation cycle rather than the cold. Do those four things and everything else — projector choice, software, mat — is happily reversible. Skip any one of them and you own a very expensive storage shed with a golf screen in it. FAQQuick answers

What is the minimum shed size for a golf simulator?

Per retailer planning guides, roughly 10 ft wide × 14–15 ft deep × 8.5–9 ft high is the absolute floor, and Carl's Place calls 12×20 ft the minimum viable shed footprint. Comfortable is 12–14 ft W × 16–18 ft D × 9–10 ft H; ideal is 14–16 ft W × 18–25 ft D × 10 ft H. Radar launch monitors like the Mevo+ push the depth requirement toward the high end.

Will an 8-foot ceiling work for a golf simulator shed?

It's the bare minimum, not a plan. Carl's Place says 8 ft fits their shortest (7.7 ft) enclosure but usually means no driver — you'll be limited to wedges and shorter irons for most golfers. Shop Indoor Golf calls 8 ft the absolute minimum and 9 ft+ optimal. If you want to swing every club, build to 9 ft workable, 10 ft comfortable.

Is an uninsulated shed OK for a golf simulator?

Only if you treat it like a garage, not a room: forum consensus and retailer guidance agree that repeated warm-cold-warm cycling condenses moisture on electronics and optics — that cycling, not cold itself, is the killer, and projector bulbs are the most sensitive part. Either insulate and climate-control (a 12,000 BTU mini-split handles 400–500 sq ft), or remove the electronics between sessions and let them reach room temperature before powering on. In humid climates, add a dehumidifier so the impact screen doesn't mildew.

Do I need a permit for a backyard golf simulator shed?

It depends entirely on your municipality and the shed's size — many jurisdictions exempt small sheds, but a 12×20+ building with electrical typically triggers permitting. Check your local building code before ordering; some prefab vendors offer permitting assistance.

Can I use a golf simulator shed in winter?

Yes, if it's insulated and heated. A ductless mini-split (~12,000 BTU for a 400–500 sq ft space, roughly $500–$1,200 for DIY-friendly units) both heats and cools, and keeping the interior conditioned avoids the condensation cycling that damages electronics. A space heater can warm the room but does nothing between sessions — the mini-split's steady baseline is what protects the gear.

Does a radar launch monitor really need more room than a camera one?

Yes, meaningfully. Radar units sit behind the ball: FlightScope's specs put the Mevo+ at ~7–9 ft behind the tee plus 8–13 ft of ball flight (~16–21 ft total), the Garmin R10 needs ~6–8 ft behind the tee, and the Full Swing KIT ~10 ft. Camera units sit beside the ball: SkyTrak+ quotes ~10×10×10 ft minimum and the Bushnell Launch Pro sits ~2 ft to the side with 8–12 ft to the screen. In a tight shed, camera units are the space-saver pick.

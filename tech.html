Buying Guide — Distance Devices

Golf Rangefinder vs GPS: Which Should You Buy?

One shoots an exact number to the pin. One paints the whole hole at a glance. Here's how to pick the right tool for your game — and your wallet.

Start With the Question You Ask Most

Every gadget in this section exists to answer one question — how far? — and nearly every buying mistake comes from picking a device before you know how you like that question answered. A laser rangefinder tells you the exact distance to the thing you aim it at, which is almost always the flag. A GPS watch or handheld tells you how far you are from mapped points on the hole: front, middle, and back of the green, plus the carry over trouble. One is a sniper scope. The other is a map.

Which one you'll actually use comes down to temperament as much as handicap. If you want the precise number to a pin you can see — and you're willing to stand still and aim at it — the laser wins. If you want a yardage with a glance, no line of sight and no fiddling while your group waits, GPS wins, and it's the friendlier pick for beginners, who get more out of front-middle-back than an exact pin number they'll miss by twenty yards anyway. Our full rangefinder vs GPS breakdown walks the whole decision, including why plenty of serious golfers eventually carry both.

If the watch route wins, don't overspend. A sub-$200 watch now delivers front, middle, and back yardages, mapped hazards and layups, automatic course detection, and tens of thousands of preloaded courses out of the box — our guide to GPS watches under $200 sorts out which ones do all of that without asking for a monthly fee.

Going Laser? Three Forks in the Road

Decide on a laser and you're not done deciding. Three smaller questions follow, and each one has a wrong answer that costs real money.

  • Slope or not. Slope-compensated yardage adjusts for uphill and downhill shots, and it genuinely sharpens club selection on hilly ground. It's also prohibited in competition — so the only safe slope unit to own is one where slope switches off, ideally with a visible indicator that proves it. Our slope vs non-slope explainer covers who actually benefits and who's better off with the simpler, cheaper device.
  • How much to spend. The quiet secret of budget lasers: on the distances you actually hit — inside about 150 yards, on flat ground — they read within roughly a yard of units costing three or four times as much. The gap is real, but it lives at long range, in pin acquisition, and in display quality, not in the number you play. Our under-$100 rangefinder guide maps exactly where the price gap shows up and where it doesn't.
  • Whether your hands cooperate. If the flagstick swims in the eyepiece and you get three different readings in a row, no cheaper laser fixes that. You need true optical image stabilization, which physically steadies the picture — not a vibration "buzz," which merely confirms a lock it may never get. Our stabilized rangefinder guide for shaky hands separates the two technologies, because confusing them is an expensive mistake.

The Subscription Trap

Golf tech has discovered software pricing, and it isn't printing that on the box. Some devices you buy once and own everything, forever. Others hold real features behind a monthly or yearly fee, and the marketing does its best to blur which is which until after checkout.

The pattern is worth learning before you spend a dollar:

  • GPS watches are mostly honest at the budget end. Shot Scope, Bushnell, and TecTecTec models are fully subscription-free — every feature is yours for good. Garmin's core on-course yardages are free too, but an optional membership (around $99 a year) gates extras like green contours and aerial imagery.
  • Launch monitors are where it bites hardest. One popular unit needs roughly $200 a year to unlock its full data and simulator features, which means the sticker price is only the down payment. Meanwhile the no-subscription radar pucks hand over their numbers with no meter running.

The defense is one habit: price year two. Before buying anything with an app, ask what numbers you keep if you never pay again. Every guide on this page flags which models are subscription-free and which quietly aren't.

Numbers in the Garage

A launch monitor is the first home-practice purchase that actually changes how you practice — provided you buy it as what it is: a practice tool, not a TrackMan. Two honest catches come standard at this price. First, several of the most-recommended units list above $500 and only dip under the line on a good sale day, so "under $500" often means "under $500 if you're patient." Second, the physics are budget physics: spin is estimated rather than measured, and radar units read short indoors when you're hitting into a net a few feet away.

None of that makes them a bad buy. What you're really purchasing is carry distance and gapping — knowing what your 7-iron actually flies, not what you tell people it flies — and that single honest number is worth more than a hundred decimal-place data fields you'll never look at. Our launch monitor under $500 guide matches the device type to your space (radar wants room outdoors; a tight garage leans camera or hybrid), separates the always-under-$500 picks from the sale-day pretenders, and names the cheapest route to numbers you can trust.
